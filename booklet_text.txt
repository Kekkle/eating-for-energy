Eating for Energy

Learn how to eat for optimal energy

We all need energy to live.  Our bodies need energy for lots of different functions. We need energy in muscles when we move and exercise and our brains need a constant supply of energy for thinking, studying and reading.  We even need energy to digest our food.

Energy gives us vitality, and a good supply of energy helps us to feel enthusiastic about doing things.  Low energy on the other hand can lead to a bad mood and lack of motivation.  Lack of energy is likely to make us feel like we are running on a half empty battery.

Our bodies make energy from raw materials provided by the food we eat. There are three main food groups – carbohydrate, protein and fat – that provide us with this fuel for energy.  These are called ‘macronutrients’ because we consume them in the largest quantities.

Eating the right foods in the right amounts can have a positive effect on energy levels, and even small changes can make a big difference. This booklet provides steps and tips about how to eat to get more energy.  When you have read it, see if you can pick and implement two actions that will help boost your energy levels (see Step 9).

Carbohydrates

Did you know that carbohydrate is our body’s favourite source of fuel? All

carbohydrate foods that we eat are broken down into a sugar called glucose which is released into our blood and used by cells to make energy.

There are lots of reasons why we might lack energy, and sometimes a lack of energy can be due to a combination of things. However, balancing levels of glucose in our blood is one of the most important factors in maintaining energy levels.  If levels of glucose are irregular and poorly controlled, this can result in feeling low in energy, tired and irritable.

“If you want to have more energy and feel more motivated to do things like trying exercise, focussing on blood glucose balance is a great place to start.  By eating the right foods in the right amounts, you can quickly feel better about yourself.”            – Andrea Carroll Langan, Registered Nutritionist

Step 1: Reduce processed/refined carbohydrates and sugary foods and drinks

Certain carbohydrates are better at supporting balanced blood glucose and energy than others. Simple sugars and refined carbohydrates (biscuits, chocolate, sweets, white bread, white pasta, white rice and breakfast cereals) are digested and absorbed quickly into the bloodstream causing a sharp rise in blood glucose.

The foods above are all examples of simple sugars and refined carbohydrates

Watch this video to understand more about blood sugar control

Too much glucose in the blood can be damaging to our bodies, so levels are tightly controlled by a hormone called insulin.  If blood glucose rises too quickly such as after a sugary snack or a meal containing a lot of carbohydrate, too much insulin may be released which removes too much glucose from the blood, causing a blood glucose ‘dip’ or ‘low’.  This can result in low energy and mood as well cravings for more sugar or sweet foods.  Avoiding refined carbohydrates and sugary foods can help avoid blood glucose highs and lows and ensure a steadier flow of energy.

Starchy and unrefined carbohydrate (brown rice, wholemeal pasta and bread, as well as starchy fruits and vegetables such as banana and sweet potato) have a different effect.  These are more complex carbohydrates which take the body longer to digest and break down into glucose, ensuring a slower release of glucose into the blood and more stable blood glucose levels.

The foods above are all examples of starchy, unrefined or complex carbohydrates

Carbohydrate and weight

If we eat more carbohydrate and sugar than we need for energy, the body will store it in our liver and muscles to be used for energy at another time.  However, the body can only store a limited amount, and any that is not needed is converted to fat. Therefore, eating too much carbohydrate and sugar can cause us to gain weight.

It isn’t just about how we look from the outside – poor diet choices such as sugary foods, junk and processed foods can lead to fat building up inside of our bodies around organs such as the heart and liver(4).  This means we can be a normal weight and still have high levels of fat, which are bad for our health.

You probably know that foods such as chocolate, cakes and puddings all contain a lot of sugar but there are also sugars ‘lurking’ where you may not expect to find them.  Even some foods that most people expect to be healthy can actually contain high levels of ‘free’ sugar.  Do the following examples surprise you?

Step 2: Eat protein with every meal and snack

Protein is found in foods such as chicken, fish, eggs, cheese, yogurt, nuts, seeds, soy products and beans. It forms the ‘building blocks’ for muscles and tissues, enzymes for digestion and antibodies for our immune system. It is vital for growth and repair of tissues, so it is important for healthy hair and skin. It can also be turned into a source of energy.

Protein and blood sugar

Including protein with every meal and snack can help improve energy levels.  This is because protein is digested more slowly than carbohydrate and sits in our stomachs for longer. For this reason, meals and snacks containing protein alongside carbohydrate are digested more slowly than carbohydrate eaten alone and support better blood glucose balance and energy levels.

Because protein remains in our stomach for longer while it is digested, it also helps us feel fuller for longer, reducing hunger and making us less likely to crave sugary foods and chocolate.

Try this! Include a portion of protein with every meal and snack. Each of the following is one portion of protein:

A piece of fish or meat the size and thickness of your hand or the size of a deck of cards

1 matchbox size piece of cheese

2 eggs

1 tablespoon of peanut butter

1 carton of plain, natural yogurt

Step 3: Eat healthy fats

Fats are a really important part of our diet and are needed for many things including forming the structure of our cells and the function of our nerves and senses – they provide the starting point for making hormones and are important for healthy skin(5). They also provide a rich source of energy.

Some fats known as ‘healthy fats’ or ‘essential fats’ have several health benefits including supporting brain function and mental health. These types of fat are found in foods including nuts, seeds, fish such as salmon and mackerel, avocados and olive oil.

Good fats also keep us feeling fuller for longer. Similar to protein, combining a healthy fat with carbohydrates in meals and snacks helps to slow the rate at which glucose is released into the bloodstream – helping to ensure balanced blood glucose levels and a steady supply of energy.

Step 4: ‘Eat a rainbow’ every day

Vitamins and minerals are important nutrients which are needed to turn fuel (from carbohydrate, protein and fat) into energy.  Without these, the process does not work, leaving us low in energy.

Fresh fruit and vegetables are a rich source of these ‘micronutrients’. Different types and colours provide different vitamins and minerals, so the more different coloured fruits and vegetables you can eat every day, the wider the range of nutrients you will include.

Try this! If you are currently eating one piece of fruit or vegetable each day, try and increase this to 3.  If you are currently eating 2, try and increase this to 4, and so on. Step 5: Always Eat Breakfast

How many of us skip breakfast because we don’t have time in the morning? This can lead to hunger, cravings, low energy and mood swings for the rest of the day. Eating a well-balanced breakfast helps to balance blood glucose levels and provides important energy for learning and activities during the day.

Try to eat breakfast soon after getting up.  Low blood glucose levels in the morning can cause you to feel tired later on(1). Like all meals, breakfast should ideally include a complex carbohydrate, a source of protein and a healthy fat.

Step 6: Snack healthily

If you feel hungry or low in energy between meals it is useful to have a snack, so think about choosing a snack which is going to support your blood glucose balance and give you plenty of energy until your next meal.  Try to avoid reaching for chocolate and sugary foods which may give you a short burst of energy but can leave you feeling tired and irritable.  Always aim to combine a good source of protein with a complex carbohydrate to support your energy levels.

Try this!  Instead of eating sugary snacks and chocolate in the middle of the afternoon, choose an apple or a pear with a handful of nuts.  Or have a lower sugar bar handy in your school bag. This type of snack will help maintain your energy levels until dinner time.   If you do fancy something sweet like chocolate or a biscuit, remember to think, where’s my protein? You could try chocolate covered seeds or even have a few nuts with your biscuit or chocolate which will help to avoid the blood glucose ‘roller coaster’.

Remember that the small achievable swaps and changes can still make a difference to your energy levels and health overall. Other healthy snack options include:

2 tablespoons of Philadelphia cheese with carrot sticks

Aldi Cashew Crunch Raw Fruit and Nut Bar

A plain yogurt with a handful of nuts

A banana with a handful of nuts

Celery sticks and 2 tablespoons of sugar free peanut butter

Tip! If you fancy a small amount of chocolate or a sweet dessert, eat this directly after a well-balanced meal when your stomach is full.  This will slow the absorption of glucose into your blood and help avoid sugar highs and lows.

Step 7: Be cautious of food myths and fads

It is important to be aware of food ‘myths’, ‘trends’ and ‘fads’ that you may see on TV or social media, as a lot of this information can be incorrect or inaccurate or just not suitable for everyone. For example, Intermittent Fasting or Time Restricted Feeding (an eating pattern that cycles between periods of eating and not eating) has become popular.  This way of eating can be good for some people, however, it is not suitable for everyone.  For example, if you scored more than 3 for the questionnaire on page 3 and have imbalanced blood glucose, this way of eating may not make you feel great.

Fad diets can limit the amount of food that you eat, or a specific diet might involve the exclusion of a complete food group.  This can mean you are missing out on important nutrients which in turn can make you tired and irritable.  Eating a healthy, balanced and varied diet, as well as keeping active, will help you have a healthy attitude towards food.

Step 8: Make sure you are well hydrated

Feeling tired and low in energy can be a sign that you are not drinking enough water.  About 60% of our body is made of water(2) and we can only last for a few days without it.  We lose more water through sweat when we exercise and when the weather is hot.  Drinking more water can help you feel more energetic and improve your skin.

You can tell how hydrated you are by the colour of your urine. Your urine should be pale yellow like the colour of a Norfolk straw field, rather than dark yellow like Norfolk apple juice!

Try this! Plain water is the best no-sugar thirst quencher! Try to drink 6-8 glasses or 1.2 litres of water every day.  If you find this hard to do, try one of the following:

Caffeine in drinks

Tea, coffee, cola and energy drinks contain caffeine which may interfere with blood sugar levels, energy and sleep.  Caffeine causes our blood glucose levels to rise and stay higher for longer(3).

Sugar in drinks

Drinks such as soda, pop, cola and lemonade contain a lot of hidden sugar, which just like sugar found in sweets and refined carbohydrate can cause a sharp rise in blood glucose and a blood glucose ‘roller coaster’ (see Step 1).

Remember from above - no more than 5% of our daily energy should come from sugars, which is equal to about 30g. One 330ml can of Coke contains 35g or 9 teaspoons/cubes of sugar, so this would be more than your whole day’s recommended intake in just one drink!

Diet drinks

It is easy to think that ‘NO added sugar’, ‘zero sugar’ and ‘sugar-free’ drinks are a healthy alternative.  These drinks usually contain little or no sugar BUT may contain artificial sweeteners to make them taste sweet.  These sweeteners such as Saccharine, Aspartame and Acesulfame may not be good for our health.

Energy drinks

Energy drinks often contain caffeine and sugar which might give you a short burst of energy but may also make you feel tired afterwards.

Fruit juice

Juices made from 100% natural fruit juice do contain some beneficial nutrients such as vitamins and minerals, however can still be high in sugar.  These should be drunk in small amounts or diluted with water.
