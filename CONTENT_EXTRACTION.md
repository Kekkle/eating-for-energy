## Topic 1: Where Does Energy Come From? (Animation)

The webinar includes an animation explaining energy sources. Key concepts to quiz on:

Text from “Where does energy come from” animation: Where do we get our energy from. Every cell in our body requires energy to function. And its necessary for every body process. Without energy we cannot survive. So where do we get it from? Our energy comes form the food we eat. Three main food groups or macro nutrients provide fuel for the body, carbs, protein, and fats. Carbs provide main energy source particularly for the brain and are used preferentially over protein and fats by the body. 

- Food provides energy
- The three macronutrients: carbohydrates, protein, and fat
- Carbohydrates are the body's preferred energy source

## Topic 2: Blood Sugar Regulation (Animation)

The webinar includes an animation explaining how blood sugar is regulated.

Text from “Blood sugar Regulation Animation”: When we eat carbs such as bread, pasta, cereal, sweet foods such as biscuits and cakes, and even fruits or starchy veg, they are broken down easily by the body into a sugar called glucose . Glucose is used by the cells of the body to make energy. To much glucose in the bloodstream is toxic to the  body , therefore the levels are tightly regulated by the hormone insulin. After we eat, the leve of glucose in the blood rises and insulin is released form the pancreas in order to bring blood sugar levels within normal limits. Insulin works by instructing the cells to uptake glucose so it can be safely stored out of the bloodstream to be used for energy when required. If blood sugar rises too rapidly, for example after a sugary meal or  heavy in carbs, too much insulin is released as a protective measure causing a blood sugar dip. This dip is where we have low energy, mood and cravings to eat the first sugary thing we can get a hold of. Thus the cycle starts again. These highs and lows are often referred to as the blood sugar roller coaster. By keeping tighter control of you blood sugar ;levels, that is by keeping the highs and lows associated with fluctuation energy levels, you may start to notice a more steady energy throughout the day and many more associated health benefits. 

### The Importance of Blood Sugar Regulation (slide 9)

- Maintain energy for school, sport, and daily life
- Exploiting marginal gains in sport (ability to do more for longer or with higher intensity)
- Sport longevity; reduced risk of insulin resistance and chronic illness
- Glucose is stored in liver and muscle cells
- Excess glucose is stored in adipose (fat) cells

## Topic 3: Establish a Stable Base (slide 10)

**The basic principles include:**

- Aim for 3 regular meals and 1-3 snacks
- Have balanced meals -- this means inclusion of all three food groups (protein, carbs, fats)
- Eat whole foods 80% of the time (think veggies, non-processed meats, whole grains, etc.)
- With veggies and fruits, consider variety and colour!
- Don't forget fluids!

**The four food groups highlighted:**

- Protein (illustrated with salmon, hand portion guide)
- Veg and fruit (illustrated with salad bowl, fruit salad)
- Smart carbs (illustrated with fist portion, potatoes)
- Healthy fats (illustrated with avocado, olive oil, coconut oil)

## Topic 5: Nutrition for Performance (slides 13-14)

### The "Food First" Approach (slide 14)

From base to top:

1. **Total Energy Intake** -- Eat enough energy for total daily needs, as well as sports needs
2. **Macro & Micro Nutrients** -- Consume adequate protein, carbs and fats -- as well as fluids!

## The 8 Simple Steps (slides 16-25)

### Step 1: Get Choosy with Carbs (slide 17)

**Key points:**

- Include adequate fibre in your diet (think veg, fruits, legumes!)
- Swap refined carbohydrates (e.g., white bread) for whole grain alternatives
- Limit consumption of sugary foods
- Limit sugary drinks such as cola (consider diet options?)
- Beware hidden sugars

**Tips:**

- Add fresh lime and mint to sparkling/soda water
- Soak oats overnight, top with berries!
- Choose wholegrain bread instead of white
- Caution with marketing claims
- Consider using a food scanner app

---

### Step 2: Eat a Source of Protein with Every Meal (slide 18)

**Key points:**

- Protein supports cellular growth, renewal, repair; muscle building, hormones, immune cells
- Takes longer for the body to break down than carbohydrates
- Provides gradual energy release and satiety
- Combine protein with carbohydrates
- Supports bone health and muscle tissue (e.g., collagen is a main building block of bone and muscle)

**Tips:**

- Add seeds/nuts and fruit to plain yoghurt
- Add a spoonful of peanut butter to a smoothie
- Choose high-protein yogurts (like Greek yogurt) over lower-protein options

---

### Step 3: Consume Health-Promoting Fats (slide 19)

**Key points:**

- Eating an adequate amount of fats is important for health!
- Cell membrane structure
- Absorbing and storing specific vitamins (A, D, E and K)
- Modulate inflammation and glucose metabolism
- Slows the rate sugars are absorbed into the bloodstream
- May add to feelings of satiety, especially when combined with fibre

**Tips:**

- Add olive oil to vegetables
- Add coconut oil to smoothies
- Combine fats with carbs

---

### Step 4: Hydrate! (slide 20)

**Key points:**

- How to tell you're dehydrated: urine colour, weight (1kg loss = 1L fluid), cap refill, dry lips, thirst
- Dehydration can impact blood sugar regulation
- Being well hydrated improves:
  - Concentration
  - Decision making
  - CV (cardiovascular) efficiency
  - Temperature regulation
  - Minimises stress
  - Transport of nutrients in the body

**Tips:**

- Carbs/electrolytes enhance the absorption of water and fuel working muscles
- Make a DIY sports drink

---

### Step 5: Start the Day with Breakfast (slide 21)

**Key points:**

- Eating a nutritious breakfast can have a stabilising effect on energy production throughout the day
- Skipping breakfast signals a chain of events which MAY lead to increased hunger and cravings, resulting in over eating
- Skipping breakfast may also result in low energy and mood swings
- Aim to have breakfast at a time that works best for you
- Be curious about how you feel on the days you eat breakfast (or skip breakfast!). Notice changes in energy or mood.

**Tips:**

- Add a fruit or veg to your breakfast
- Add cottage cheese or Greek yogurt to pancake mix for a higher protein option
- Have a breakfast smoothie if short on time

---

### Step 6: Consider Nutritious Snacks Between Meals (slide 22)

**Key points:**

- Eating small, nutritious snacks between meals can be a good way to keep blood sugar levels stable
- Think 80/20 rule
- If eating 3 large meals a day leaves you feeling full or low in energy, experiment with smaller meals and a few light snacks
- Minimises stress on the body caused by hunger and low blood sugar
- Consider the timing of your snacks (not too close to your next meal)

**Tips:**

- Have snack bars on hand
- Crackers / dips / seeds
- Protein-based crisps
- Veg from the allotment?

---

### Step 7: Adjust Consumption of Energizing Drinks (slide 23)

**Key points:**

- Drinks containing caffeine act as a stimulant (encouraging the release of stress hormones into the bloodstream)
- Short-lived high followed by an energy crash
- Caffeine is a diuretic (dehydration can impact blood sugar)
- Even slight dehydration can lead to a decrease in concentration/performance
- Herbal teas do not activate the stress response

**Tips:**

- Try green tea: shown to lessen anxiety and improve mental focus
- Use infuser bottles

**DIY sports drinks** -- concept introduced:

- Fuel working muscles
- Electrolytes: speed re-hydration, maintain fluid balance, and replace salts

---

### Step 8: Move Your Body! (slide 24)

**Key points:**

- Regular physical activity helps control blood sugar levels
- Muscles use sugar for energy, helping to lower levels in the blood
- Regular exercise helps your body use insulin more efficiently

**Tips:**

- Avoid over-exercising -- it causes stress
- Get the balance right and factor in other life loads

---

### Step 9 (Bonus): Relax and De-Stress (slide 25)

**Key points:**

- Stress can come in many forms (emotional, psychological, environmental)
- Stress can increase hunger and food cravings
- Chronic (long-term) stress can have a negative impact on the body
- Finding ways to relax and unwind is vital

**Tips:**

- Find YOUR relaxation medium
- Build up from 10 minutes
- Mindful eating

## Summary of the 9 Steps (slide 27)

1. Get choosy about carbohydrate sources
2. Eat a source of protein with every meal
3. Consume health-promoting fats
4. Hydrate!
5. Start the day with breakfast
6. Consider nutritious snacks between meals
7. Adjust consumption of energizing drinks
8. Move your body!
9. Relax and de-stress
